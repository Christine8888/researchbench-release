ResearchBench: Evaluating AI Agents on End-To-End Astrophysics Research Paper Replication

Christine Ye1, Sihan Yuan1, Suchetha Cooray1, Steven Dillmann1, Ian L. V. Roque1, Dalya Baron1, Philipp Frank1, Sergio Martin-Alvarez1, Nolan Koblischke2, Frank J. Qu1, Diyi Yang1, Risa Wechsler1, Ioana Ciucă1
1Stanford University 2University of Toronto
Paper Code Dataset

ResearchBench evaluates AI agents on end-to-end research paper replication. For each paper, we supply the manuscript, dataset, and execution metadata, and a complete set of author-written tasks.

Summary

Can AI agents conduct scientific research? Current benchmarks test either domain knowledge or coding skills, but not both. Here, we introduce ResearchBench, a benchmark evaluating AI agents on complete replications of peer-reviewed astrophysics papers.

Agents must reproduce papers' core contributions—from data analysis to theoretical calculations—and are scored on result accuracy. Even frontier models achieve only ~20% success, revealing significant gaps in agents' ability to conduct autonomous research.

The ResearchBench Dataset

We curate 22 reproducible astrophysics papers decomposed into 121 expert-written tasks that:

  1. Cover complete research workflows
  2. Require both domain expertise and coding ability
  3. Enable objective, automated grading

Tasks range from simple data queries (minutes) to complex theoretical implementations (days), enabling partial credit for incomplete replications.

Despite saturating knowledge benchmarks, frontier models struggle with ResearchBench's integrated challenges. Human evaluations reveal agents understand tasks reasonably well but fail at execution—making mathematical errors, mishandling data, and producing incorrect implementations.
